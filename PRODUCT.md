# Product

## Register

product

## Platform

web

## Users

ARV Finance is a lender. The people who use this app are its borrower-clients: real-estate investors — **mostly wholesalers and fix-and-flip operators** — running deals across a handful of active MSAs (Denver, Miami, San Diego, LA, SF, Port St. Lucie). Most users are "ARV clients," meaning ARV is involved in their financing or their deals; an elevated subscription tier is effectively what makes someone a client, so access and identity are tied together. They arrive mid-work — sizing up a property, sourcing or posting a deal, lining up a contractor, or checking in with the community — and they expect the tool to already know their market.

Two secondary audiences share the surface: vendors and contractors who list in the directory and get mentioned across deals and posts, and ARV itself, which broadcasts announcements through the mastermind. A single public page (Home) acts as a light one-page marketing layer for people who aren't inside yet.

## Product Purpose

One platform that does four jobs for ARV's clients, with no single job dominating: **browse property intelligence** (SFR transactions, companies, and code-violation records by MSA), **work the deal marketplace** (post and browse wholesale / agent / sold / REO deals, request contact, make offers), **find vendors** (a community directory alongside a rich activity feed), and **belong to the mastermind** (a Slack-style real-time community of fellow clients, plus announcements from ARV).

Success is that a client can run the data, deal, vendor, and community sides of their business from one place — and that the intelligence and access here are good enough that being an ARV client is worth more because of it. The app's job is to deepen the lending relationship by being the most useful thing a flipper or wholesaler in these markets can log into.

## Positioning

ARV lends to and works deals alongside the exact investors it serves, so it can offer data, deals, vendors, and community that generic real-estate tools can't — assembled in one place and informed by first-hand experience with these clients and these markets. The lending relationship is the moat: proprietary market intelligence on one side, a captive, high-intent community of fellow ARV clients on the other.

## Brand Personality

Two things held together: the **trusted insider** and the **sharp analyst**. It should look and feel like a platform built by people who have funded and closed these deals — credible, in-the-know, on the client's side — while delivering genuinely data-driven insight rather than decoration. Trustworthy enough that a paying client feels taken care of; sharp enough that the numbers feel authoritative. Professional but human; never cold, never toy.

## Anti-references

Four things to stay away from. The **generic AI/SaaS template** (cream backgrounds, gradient hero, identical icon-heading-text card grids, tracked-uppercase eyebrows). The **cluttered legacy real-estate portal** (dated, banner-ad busy, cramped MLS-style tables). **Cold enterprise or bank software** (sterile, corporate, feels like filling out a mortgage form). And **consumer-toy playfulness** (cartoonish, over-animated, unserious) — it undercuts credibility with people moving real money.

## Design Principles

- **Earn trust, then show the numbers.** These are paying clients; the surface has to read as credible and cared-for before it impresses with data. Both at once — neither alone.
- **Proprietary intelligence, served better than anyone.** Lean on what ARV uniquely knows from financing these deals; surface context and data a generic tool can't, and present it so the insight is obvious.
- **One product, four jobs, each native to its job.** Data should search like Zillow/Redfin, the mastermind should work like Slack, deals and vendors like a focused marketplace and feed — all plainly the same product, never a bundle of mismatched tools.
- **Borrow familiar affordances; don't reinvent.** Product-focused and deliberately simpler than a Bloomberg terminal. Use known patterns so clients are never taught a new way to search, chat, or filter. Even the future user dashboard stays simple.
- **Credible, never sterile or toy.** Steer between the cold-bank look and the consumer-toy look on every screen; the answer is always professional and human.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No formal WCAG target for now — sensible, standard defaults (legible contrast, keyboard-usable controls, visible focus states). Revisit if a specific client need or requirement emerges.
